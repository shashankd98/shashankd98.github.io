About Me

I am a fourth year Ph.D. student in the Astronomy Department at Yale University, working with Frank van den Bosch and Priya Natarajan. Prior to this, I was a post-bacc researcher at the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, where I worked with Monica Valluri. I received by bachelor's and master's degrees in physics from the Indian Institute of Science.

My research encompasses various aspects of computational galaxy dynamics and evolution, with an emphasis on black hole-galaxy coevolution and constraining the nature of dark matter. For more information, please check out my research page or my publications.

Outside of research, I am passionate about scientific outreach, and regularly host events and public nights at the Leitner Family Observatory and Planetarium. I am an active member of Yale Science Communication, a graduate student organization where we present public talks in libraries and bars in the New Haven area. My other hobbies include rock climbing, dancing, and playing the violin.

Research

Phase-space origins of dynamical friction, core stalling, and buoyancy
In this project, we have studied the trajectories of massive perturbers (e.g. black holes) in systems with a central core. Using a combination of N-body simulations and analytic arguments from kinetic theory, we have shown that the phase-space distribution function of the system is ultimately what determines the inspiral rate and direction. Stalling and buoyancy occur due to plateus and inflections in the distribution function, respectively. Surprisingly, these dynamics do not occur in self-interacting dark matter halos, providing a new avenue to constrain the nature of dark matter.

Dipole instabilities in gravitational N-body systems
We explored the stability of spherical isotropic systems with an inflection in the distribution function, which commonly occurs in double power-law density profiles with a sharp transition from the outer to inner slopes. Using N-body simulations and linear response theory, we have shown that such systems are susceptible to an unstable dipole mode. This rotating dipole grows over time until saturating into a long-livesd soliton that sloshes back and forth through the center. This instability is the analogue of the bump-on-tail instability in plasma physics.

Redshift evolution of the black hole mass - stellar mass relation
We analyzed the ASTRID and TNG cosmological simulations and the Dark Sage semi-analytic model to track the evolution of the black hole mass - stellar mass relation across time. Due to differences in the subgrid models for BH seeding, feedback, and accretion, these datasets all show different evolutionary tracks. We show that ultimately, the ratio between the specific BH accretion rate and the host galaxy’s specific star formation rate is what controls the trajectory of an individual source in the black hole mass - stellar mass plane. This ratio depends on the implementation of subgrid dynamical friction and the nature of quenching due to AGN feedback.

Deprojection and stellar dynamical modeling of edge-on barred galaxies
We developed a new method to deproject edge-on galaxies, focusing on barred galaxies with boxy-peanut bulges. This method was tested on mock data from simulations, and we recovered the density within !5% of the true value. We then applied this deprojection technique in the Schwarzschild modeling algorithm, and recovered the galaxy’s bar pattern speed to within 10% error.
